### About the series

These essays began with a question that circulates in studios and lecture theatres: now that machines can produce graphic outputs, product renders, interface screens, and working prototypes in seconds, what is left for designers? The series answers by examining what design actually is when it is examined closely. It finds a practice whose most consequential work happens upstream of any artefact, in the constituting of problems; a process that is irreducibly argumentative, conducted through a web of issues that must be opened, contested, and closed by judgment rather than by criterion; an intervention into a future the intervention itself remakes; and a profession defined, in the end, not by what it makes but by what it is answerable for. The machines take a side in a quarrel the field thought it had abandoned. These essays take the other side, and explain why the consequences live there. They draw on Horst Rittel, Herbert Simon, Gabriela Goldschmidt, Vinod Goel, and Hans-Georg Gadamer, and they are written for practitioners, students, and anyone deciding what design education and design teams should now protect.


### The essays

I. [The Brief Always Arrives Wrong](/articles/the-brief-always-arrives-wrong/)
On why the profession will not run out of work simply because machines have learned to make things.
The opening essay dismantles the picture of design as a fixed inventory of artefacts waiting to be produced, and locates the profession's real work upstream of any deliverable, in establishing what should be made, for whom, and why.

II. [The Web of Issues](/articles/the-web-of-issues/)
On design as argumentation, and why the process is the work.
Drawing on Horst Rittel, this essay argues that designing is the traversal of a web of issues, positions, and arguments, and that closing an issue is never a calculation but a judgment, political in nature, for which someone must answer.

III. [The Quarrel That AI Reopened](/articles/the-quarrel-that-ai-reopened/)
On why a fifty-year-old argument in design theory has suddenly become a practical question.
The founding dispute between Herbert Simon and Rittel, abandoned rather than resolved, returns with force: generative AI is Simon's account of design built and shipping, which makes Rittel's objections newly urgent.

IV. [Two Stopping Rules](/articles/two-stopping-rules/)
On how designers decide that the designing is done.
Simon said stop at good enough; Rittel said there is no rule, only stopping you can defend; and the tools have quietly installed a third rule that neither proposed: stop when the output looks finished.

V. [The Resolution of the Argument](/articles/the-resolution-of-the-argument/)
On sketches, prompts, and the level of abstraction at which design arguments live.
Through Gabriela Goldschmidt and Vinod Goel, this essay shows that the web of issues is not medium-neutral: ambiguous representations keep issues open, and a practice of text in and renders out conducts its arguments in a channel that can only close them.
